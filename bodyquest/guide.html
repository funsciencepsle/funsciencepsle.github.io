PSLE Science Study Guide: Human Respiratory & Circulatory Systems

Primary 5 — Systems Theme (MOE Syllabus)

PART A — THE RESPIRATORY SYSTEM

1. What is the Respiratory System For?

The respiratory system lets us breathe. Its two jobs: - Take in oxygen (which the body needs). - Remove carbon dioxide (a waste gas the body does not need).

⚠️ Trap alert — "breathe" vs "respire": - Breathing = taking air in and pushing air out (a physical action). - Respiration = releasing energy from digested food, using oxygen, inside every cell. - Don't mix them up! We breathe to supply the oxygen needed for respiration.

2. Parts of the Respiratory System (in order)

Air travels in this path:

Nose → Windpipe (trachea) → Lungs (→ air sacs)

Part Function
Nose Air enters here. Hairs and mucus trap dust and germs; the nose also warms and moistens the air.
Windpipe (trachea) A tube that carries air from the nose down to the lungs.
Lungs Two organs where gaseous exchange takes place.
Air sacs (in the lungs) Where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves the blood.

3. Breathing In and Breathing Out

Breathing IN (inhaling) Breathing OUT (exhaling)
What enters/leaves Air with more oxygen enters Air with more carbon dioxide leaves
Chest Chest expands (gets bigger) Chest contracts (gets smaller)

⚠️ Trap alert: Inhaled air is not "pure oxygen", and exhaled air is not "pure carbon dioxide". - Inhaled air: more oxygen, less carbon dioxide. - Exhaled air: less oxygen, more carbon dioxide. - Both still contain oxygen AND carbon dioxide — the amounts just change.

4. Gaseous Exchange (the key idea!)

Inside the lungs are millions of tiny air sacs. This is where gases are swapped with the blood:

At the air sacs, oxygen passes from the air into the blood. At the same time, carbon dioxide passes from the blood into the air sacs, to be breathed out.

Why are air sacs good at their job? (common question) - There are millions of them, giving a large surface area for gaseous exchange to happen quickly.

Answer formula (memorise!):

"Oxygen from the air in the air sacs passes into the blood. Carbon dioxide from the blood passes into the air sacs and is breathed out."

PART B — THE CIRCULATORY SYSTEM

5. What is the Circulatory System For?

The circulatory system transports things around the body. It carries: - Oxygen and digested food → to all parts of the body. - Carbon dioxide and other wastes → away from the body parts (to be removed).

It is made up of the heart, the blood vessels, and the blood.

6. The Heart

  • The heart is a pump made of muscle.
  • It pumps blood to all parts of the body, without stopping.
  • When the heart beats, we can feel a pulse.

The heart pumps blood around the body through the blood vessels.

⚠️ Trap alert: The heart does not make blood or make oxygen. Its job is to pump blood so that blood can transport oxygen and food.

7. Blood Vessels

Blood flows through tiny tubes called blood vessels. Blood vessels form a network reaching every part of the body.

  • Blood leaves the heart → flows through blood vessels → reaches all body parts → returns to the heart.
  • The blood vessels are so widespread that every cell is close to one, so it can receive oxygen and food and give up its wastes.

8. What the Blood Carries

Blood is the transport liquid. As blood flows past the air sacs in the lungs and the small intestine, it picks things up; as it flows past body parts, it drops them off.

Blood picks up... ...at and delivers to
Oxygen the lungs (air sacs) all body parts
Digested food the small intestine all body parts
Carbon dioxide all body parts the lungs (to breathe out)

Key link: The blood carries oxygen from the lungs and digested food from the small intestine to all parts of the body.

9. How the Systems Work Together (very important for PSLE!)

The respiratory and circulatory systems work together with the digestive system:

  1. We breathe in → oxygen enters the air sacs in the lungs.
  2. Oxygen passes into the blood (gaseous exchange).
  3. The heart pumps the blood, which transports oxygen (and digested food from the small intestine) to all parts of the body.
  4. Body cells use the oxygen and food to release energy (respiration).
  5. This produces carbon dioxide, which passes into the blood.
  6. Blood carries the carbon dioxide back to the lungs, and we breathe it out.

⚠️ Trap alert: In a question that mentions running or exercise: - Muscles work harder → need more oxygen and more energy → we breathe faster and deeper (to take in more oxygen) and the heart beats faster (to pump blood, and hence oxygen, to the muscles more quickly).

10. Answering Techniques (Section B)

Must-use keywords: - "gaseous exchange" / "air sacs" / "large surface area" - "oxygen passes from the air sacs into the blood" - "carbon dioxide passes from the blood into the air sacs" - "the heart pumps blood" / "transports / carries" - "digested food from the small intestine" - "to all parts of the body" / "release energy"

System-link answer formula:

"Oxygen enters the blood at the air sacs. The heart pumps the blood, which transports the oxygen to all parts of the body, where it is used to release energy."

11. Quick Practice Questions

Q1. Why does the nose have hairs and produce mucus?

Answer: The hairs and mucus trap dust and germs in the air, so that cleaner air enters the lungs. (The nose also warms and moistens the air.)

Q2. Explain how oxygen in the air we breathe reaches the muscles in our legs.

Answer: Oxygen enters the air sacs in the lungs and passes into the blood. The heart pumps the blood, which transports the oxygen through the blood vessels to the muscles in the legs.

Q3. Why do we breathe faster and our heart beat faster when we run?

Answer: The muscles work harder and need more oxygen to release more energy. We breathe faster and deeper to take in more oxygen, and the heart beats faster to pump the oxygen-carrying blood to the muscles more quickly.

Q4. Why is it useful that the lungs contain millions of tiny air sacs?

Answer: The millions of air sacs give a large surface area, so that gaseous exchange (oxygen entering the blood and carbon dioxide leaving) can take place quickly.

Q5. How is exhaled air different from inhaled air?

Answer: Exhaled air contains less oxygen and more carbon dioxide than inhaled air. (Both still contain some of each — the amounts change.)

Q6. A doctor asks a patient to hold their breath. Explain why the patient soon feels the need to breathe.

Answer: While holding the breath, no fresh oxygen enters the body and carbon dioxide builds up in the blood. The body needs to take in more oxygen and remove the carbon dioxide, so the patient feels the need to breathe.

12. One-Page Revision Summary

  1. Respiratory system = breathing: take in oxygen, remove carbon dioxide.
  2. Air path: nose → windpipe → lungs → air sacs. The nose traps dust/germs and warms & moistens air.
  3. Gaseous exchange at the air sacs: oxygen → blood; carbon dioxide → air sacs → breathed out. Millions of air sacs = large surface area.
  4. Inhaled air = more oxygen; exhaled air = more carbon dioxide (never "pure").
  5. Circulatory system = transport: heart + blood vessels + blood.
  6. The heart pumps blood; blood transports oxygen (from lungs) and digested food (from small intestine) to all body parts, and carries carbon dioxide back to the lungs.
  7. Systems work together: oxygen + food → cells → release energy → carbon dioxide out.
  8. Exercise: muscles need more oxygen → breathe faster & deeper, heart beats faster.

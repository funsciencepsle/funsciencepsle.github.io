PSLE Science Study Guide: Cycles of Water

Ice, Water and Water Vapour · Cycles Theme (MOE Syllabus)

1. Water Exists in Three States

State Example How it looks
Solid Ice Fixed shape and volume
Liquid Water No fixed shape, fixed volume
Gas Water vapour No fixed shape or volume — INVISIBLE

Water changes from one state to another by gaining or losing heat (link back to the Heat topic!):

  • Melting (solid → liquid): ice gains heat at 0°C
  • Freezing (liquid → solid): water loses heat at 0°C
  • Evaporation / Boiling (liquid → gas): water gains heat
  • Condensation (gas → liquid): water vapour loses heat

⚠️ Trap alert: Water vapour is a gas and is INVISIBLE. If you can see it, it is NOT water vapour! - Steam, mist, fog and clouds are all tiny water droplets (liquid), formed when water vapour condenses. - ❌ "I can see the water vapour rising from the kettle." → ✅ "The white 'steam' seen is tiny water droplets formed when the hot water vapour condensed in the cooler air."

2. Evaporation — Liquid to Gas

Definition sentence (memorise!):

Water gains heat from the surroundings (mainly the Sun) and evaporates to become water vapour.

  • Happens slowly, at any temperature, and only at the surface of the water.
  • The Sun is the main source of heat that drives the water cycle.

Factors that increase the rate of evaporation:

Factor Why it speeds up evaporation
Higher temperature (more heat) Water gains heat at a faster rate
Larger exposed surface area More water surface is in contact with the air
Moving air / wind Moving air carries the water vapour away faster
Drier surrounding air (lower humidity) Dry air can "take in" more water vapour

Classic example: Wet clothes dry fastest when they are spread out (larger surface area), on a hot day (more heat), in a windy place (moving air).

3. Condensation — Gas to Liquid

Definition sentence (memorise!):

Water vapour in the air comes into contact with a cooler surface, loses heat, and condenses into tiny water droplets.

Condensation happens faster when the surface or surrounding air is cooler.

⚠️ Classic PSLE question — droplets on a glass of iced water:

"Water vapour in the surrounding air came into contact with the cooler outer surface of the glass. The water vapour lost heat to the cold surface and condensed into water droplets."

(The droplets come from the water vapour in the AIR, not from the water inside the glass!)

4. The Water Cycle — Stage by Stage

The Sun powers the water cycle.

  1. Evaporation: Water from seas, rivers, lakes and other water bodies gains heat from the Sun and evaporates into water vapour.
  2. Rising & cooling: The water vapour rises. High up in the sky, the surrounding air is cooler.
  3. Condensation: The water vapour loses heat to the cooler air and condenses into tiny water droplets. Millions of these droplets form clouds.
  4. Rain: The tiny droplets combine to form bigger, heavier droplets. When they become too heavy to stay up, they fall as rain.
  5. Collection: Rainwater flows back into drains, rivers, lakes and seas — and the cycle repeats.

⚠️ Trap alerts for the cycle: - Clouds do NOT "melt" to form rain. The droplets combine, become bigger and heavier, and fall as rain. - Water vapour condenses because it loses heat to the cooler air high up — always mention losing heat. - Never write "the water disappears." Write "the water evaporated and became water vapour."

Why is the water cycle important?

Water on Earth is recycled continuously. The water cycle ensures a continuous supply of fresh water (rain) for living things. Water that evaporates from the salty sea falls as fresh rainwater — this is also why we can obtain salt by evaporating seawater: the water evaporates, but the salt is left behind.

5. A Model of the Water Cycle (Closed Containers)

A sealed terrarium, or a clear plastic sheet over a cup of water in the sun, models the water cycle:

  • Water gains heat and evaporates → water vapour rises → touches the cooler plastic/lid → loses heat and condenses into droplets → droplets combine, become heavy and fall back down.

⚠️ Trap alert: In a sealed container, the total amount (mass) of water does not change — it just keeps changing state and location. If the container is open, water vapour escapes into the air and the water level decreases over time.

6. Boiling vs Evaporation (bridge to the Heat topic)

Boiling Evaporation
Speed Fast Slow
Temperature Only at boiling point (100°C for water) At any temperature
Where it happens Throughout the liquid Only at the surface

The water cycle runs on evaporation — the sea never boils!

7. Answering Techniques (Section B)

Must-use keywords: - "gains heat from the Sun/surroundings" / "loses heat to the cooler ___" - "evaporates to become water vapour" - "condenses into (tiny) water droplets" - "at a faster/slower rate" - "larger/smaller exposed surface area" - "the droplets combine and become bigger and heavier"

Evaporation answer formula:

"The water in ___ gained heat from ___ and evaporated to become water vapour at a faster/slower rate because ___."

Condensation answer formula:

"The water vapour in the air came into contact with the cooler ___. It lost heat to the ___ and condensed into water droplets."

8. Quick Practice Questions

Q1. The water level in an open pond decreased on a hot day, even though no one removed any water. Explain.

Answer: The water in the pond gained heat from the Sun and evaporated to become water vapour, which escaped into the air. So the water level decreased.

Q2. Siti hangs two identical wet towels: one folded, one spread out. Which dries faster and why?

Answer: The spread-out towel. It has a larger exposed surface area in contact with the surrounding air, so the water evaporates at a faster rate.

Q3. Why do water droplets form on the inside of a sealed plastic bag containing a plant, left in the sun?

Answer: Water from the plant and soil gained heat and evaporated to become water vapour. The water vapour came into contact with the cooler inner surface of the bag, lost heat and condensed into water droplets.

Q4. How can we obtain salt from seawater? Explain using the idea of evaporation.

Answer: Leave the seawater in a shallow container under the Sun. The water gains heat and evaporates to become water vapour, leaving the salt behind.

Q5. John says he can see water vapour coming out of a boiling kettle. Is he correct?

Answer: No. Water vapour is invisible. The white "steam" he sees is made of tiny water droplets, formed when the hot water vapour lost heat to the cooler surrounding air and condensed.

9. One-Page Revision Summary

  1. Water exists as solid (ice), liquid (water), gas (water vapour) — and changes state by gaining or losing heat.
  2. Water vapour is INVISIBLE. Steam, mist and clouds = tiny water droplets.
  3. Evaporation: water gains heat (mainly from the Sun) → water vapour. Slow, any temperature, surface only.
  4. Evaporation is faster with more heat, more wind, larger exposed surface area, drier air.
  5. Condensation: water vapour touches a cooler surface/air → loses heat → water droplets.
  6. Water cycle: evaporate → rise & cool → condense into clouds → droplets combine, get heavy → rain → collect → repeat. The Sun powers it all.
  7. Clouds don't "melt" — droplets combine and become heavier, then fall as rain.
  8. The water cycle gives Earth a continuous supply of fresh water.

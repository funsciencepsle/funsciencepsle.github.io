PSLE Science Study Guide: Heat and Its Effects

Primary 5 — Energy Theme (MOE Syllabus)

1. Heat vs Temperature (VERY IMPORTANT — common exam trap!)

Heat Temperature
What is it? A form of energy A measure of how hot or cold something is
Unit Joules (J) Degrees Celsius (°C)
Measured with — Thermometer

Key sentence to remember:

Heat is a form of energy. Temperature measures the degree of hotness of an object.

⚠️ Trap alert: Never say "the object has more temperature" or "heat is 30°C". Heat is energy; temperature is a measurement. An object at a higher temperature does not always contain more heat energy (e.g. a cup of boiling water vs a warm swimming pool — the pool has more heat energy overall!).

2. How Heat Travels

Golden rule: Heat always flows from a hotter object/place to a cooler object/place.

  • Heat flows until both objects reach the same temperature.
  • When an object gains heat, its temperature rises.
  • When an object loses heat, its temperature falls.

⚠️ Trap alert: There is NO such thing as "cold" entering an object. In exams, never write "the cold air entered the cup". Always write in terms of heat moving: - ✅ "Heat from the hot water flowed to the cooler surrounding air." - ❌ "Coldness moved into the water."

Answering formula (memorise this!):

"Heat travels from the (hotter object) to the (cooler object). The (hotter object) loses heat and its temperature decreases, while the (cooler object) gains heat and its temperature increases."

3. Effects of Heat

Effect 1: Change in Temperature

  • Gain heat → temperature increases
  • Lose heat → temperature decreases

Effect 2: Expansion and Contraction

  • When matter gains heat, it expands (increases in size/volume).
  • When matter loses heat, it contracts (decreases in size/volume).
  • This happens to solids, liquids AND gases.

⚠️ Trap alert: The mass does NOT change when something expands or contracts. Only the volume/size changes. If a question asks about mass after heating, the answer is: mass remains the same.

Real-life examples (great for open-ended answers): | Example | Explanation | |---|---| | Gaps in railway tracks / bridges | Allow the metal to expand on hot days without bending or buckling | | Overhead cables sag on hot days | The cables gain heat and expand, becoming longer | | Running hot water over a tight metal lid | The lid gains heat and expands, making it easier to open | | Thermometer liquid rises | The liquid gains heat and expands up the tube |

Effect 3: Change of State

Heat gain or heat loss can change matter from one state to another.

Heat GAINED (temperature of surroundings is higher): - Melting: solid → liquid (e.g. ice → water at 0°C) - Boiling: liquid → gas, rapidly, at boiling point (water boils at 100°C) - Evaporation: liquid → gas, slowly, at any temperature (this is why puddles dry up even on cool days)

Heat LOST (temperature of surroundings is lower): - Freezing: liquid → solid (water freezes at 0°C) - Condensation: gas → liquid (e.g. water vapour in the air touches a cold surface and forms water droplets)

⚠️ Classic PSLE question — droplets on a cold drink can:

"Water vapour in the surrounding air came into contact with the cooler outer surface of the can. The water vapour lost heat to the cold surface and condensed into water droplets."

(The droplets come from the AIR, not from inside the can!)

⚠️ Boiling vs Evaporation (compare-and-contrast favourite): | | Boiling | Evaporation | |---|---|---| | Speed | Fast | Slow | | Temperature | Only at boiling point (100°C for water) | At any temperature | | Where it happens | Throughout the liquid | Only at the surface of the liquid |

4. Conductors and Insulators of Heat

  • A good conductor of heat allows heat to pass through it quickly. (e.g. metals: copper, aluminium, iron, steel)
  • A poor conductor of heat (insulator) allows heat to pass through it slowly. (e.g. plastic, wood, rubber, styrofoam, fabric, air, water)

⚠️ Trap alert: An insulator does NOT "block" or "stop" heat. It only slows down the flow of heat. Never write "the styrofoam box stops heat from entering". Write: "Styrofoam is a poor conductor of heat, so heat from the surroundings enters the box slowly, keeping the drink cold for a longer time."

Applications table: | Object | Material | Why? | |---|---|---| | Cooking pot (body) | Metal — good conductor | Heat passes quickly from the fire to the food | | Pot handle | Plastic/wood — poor conductor | Heat travels slowly to our hand, so it does not burn us | | Winter clothing | Wool (traps air) — poor conductor | Body heat is lost to the surroundings slowly, keeping us warm | | Ice-cream cooler box | Styrofoam — poor conductor | Heat from the surroundings enters slowly, so the ice cream melts slowly |

Special point: Air is a very poor conductor of heat. Materials that trap air (wool, styrofoam, double-layered cups) are good insulators.

5. Answering Techniques for Open-Ended Questions (Section B)

Use the CER-style structure: state the science concept, then link it to the situation.

Must-use keywords (markers look for these!): - "gains heat" / "loses heat" - "heat flows/travels from ___ to ___" - "temperature increases/decreases" - "expands" / "contracts" - "good/poor conductor of heat" - "at a faster/slower rate"

Step-by-step for a typical question: 1. Identify which object is hotter and which is cooler. 2. State the direction heat flows (hotter → cooler). 3. State the effect (temperature change / expansion / change of state). 4. Link back to what the question asked ("...therefore, the lid becomes easier to open").

6. Quick Practice Questions

Q1. A metal ball cannot pass through a metal ring. Suggest one way to make the ball pass through the ring.

Answer: Cool the ball (e.g. place it in ice water). The ball loses heat and contracts, becoming smaller, so it can pass through the ring. (Alternative: heat the ring so it expands.)

Q2. Ali touched a metal spoon and a wooden spoon left in the same room. The metal spoon felt colder. Are the spoons at different temperatures? Explain.

Answer: No, both spoons are at the same temperature (room temperature). Metal is a better conductor of heat than wood, so it conducts heat away from Ali's hand at a faster rate, making it feel colder.

Q3. Why are there gaps between sections of a concrete bridge?

Answer: On hot days, the concrete gains heat and expands. The gaps allow space for the concrete to expand so that the bridge does not crack or buckle.

Q4. Water droplets formed on the mirror after Siti took a hot shower. Explain why.

Answer: The water vapour in the air came into contact with the cooler surface of the mirror. The water vapour lost heat to the mirror and condensed into water droplets.

7. One-Page Revision Summary

  1. Heat = energy (J); Temperature = degree of hotness (°C).
  2. Heat flows from hotter → cooler, until temperatures are equal.
  3. Gain heat → temperature ↑, expand, may melt/boil/evaporate.
  4. Lose heat → temperature ↓, contract, may freeze/condense.
  5. Expansion/contraction changes size, NOT mass.
  6. Good conductors (metals) transfer heat quickly; insulators (plastic, wood, air, styrofoam) transfer heat slowly — they never "block" heat.
  7. Never write "cold moves" — only heat moves!
